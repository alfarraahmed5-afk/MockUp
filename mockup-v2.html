— 01 / Ecological Landscaping

Living ground.
Precisely made.

Our landscape practice spans formal courtyard gardens to multi-hectare estate rewilding. Every scheme is anchored in ecological survey data — not aesthetic preference or catalogue selection.

01
Planting Design

Native flora. Selected by soil, not by catalogue.

Our species palettes are drawn exclusively from locally-native provenance stock, selected against a baseline soil and hydrology survey. We do not use horticultural cultivars within designated ecological zones.

Every scheme includes a five-year establishment monitoring programme with annual species-density reports delivered to the client and, where applicable, to the Local Planning Authority.

— Featured Specialism

Gravel Gardens &
Mineral Substrates

Inspired by the work of Beth Chatto and grounded in geological precision, our gravel garden designs begin with a substrate specification — not a plant list. The mineral layer is the ecology; planting is the expression of those conditions.

We source all aggregates from quarries matched to the regional geology of each site, ensuring the garden reads as genuinely native to its landscape from the outset.

  • Decomposed granite — low-fertility drainage base layer
  • Angular flint — chalk-land and Chiltern planting palettes
  • Crushed Carboniferous limestone — Welsh upland vernacular
  • Pea gravel over geotextile membrane — urban xeriscaping
  • Hoggin & compressed shale — permeable rural pathways

— 02 / Heritage Lime Restoration

Ancient stone.
Matched precisely.

For period property owners and conservation architects. Our lime work is underpinned by geological analysis, not approximation. Every mortar specification is unique to its host masonry.

— The technical case

Why mortar specification matters more than the application itself

The single most common cause of masonry failure in listed buildings is the application of an incorrect binder. Portland cement introduced into historic joints causes stress fracture, moisture entrapment, and accelerated weathering of adjacent stone faces — the very faces it was applied to protect.

Our specification process begins with an M-Class extraction — a physical sample of the original mortar matrix taken from multiple courses and zones, then analysed for aggregate grading, binder type, and hydraulic content. The results determine everything: lime classification, aggregate source, water-binder ratio, and curing regime.

We hold access to natural hydraulic lime from certified UK and French sources, including feebly-hydraulic limes specifically for the most sensitive conservation contexts. All material is supplied with full documentation for planning and conservation officer approval.

— Our method

From survey to completion: a four-stage process

01

Mortar Extraction & Analysis

Samples are extracted from multiple courses and zones. Each is described for colour, aggregate composition, hardness, and binder type — producing a full M-Class record for the building.

02

Bespoke Mix Specification

Using extraction data, we produce a mortar specification for each zone of work. Aggregate is sourced to match the original in size, angularity, and geological origin.

03

Sample Panel & Approval

Before production work begins, a sample panel is laid for visual and technical approval. For listed buildings, this is submitted to the conservation officer as consent documentation.

04

Execution & Curing

All work is carried out by our own trained operatives. Lime mortars require careful slow curing away from frost and direct sun. A written curing programme is provided with every contract.

— For conservation architects

We prepare full mortar specifications for inclusion in your tender documentation

If you are leading a heritage project requiring lime mortar specification, structural repointing, or internal plaster restoration, we can act as a specialist subcontractor or provide an independent specification document for competitive tender.

We work under NEC4 and JCT contracts and provide full material certification, MSDS documentation, and photographic site records as standard deliverables on every commission.

The Mineral Logic of a Dry Gravel Garden: substrate first, plants second

Most gravel garden failures stem from a category error: treating aggregate as a mulch, rather than the primary growing medium. When we specify a mineral substrate, we are specifying an ecology — one defined by drainage rate, pH, thermal mass, and microbial activity. The plant palette is a consequence of those conditions, not a precondition for them.
